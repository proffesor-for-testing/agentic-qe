How can this report help you?

▼
"Requirements are not an end in themselves, but a means to an end - the end of providing value to some person(s)." - Jerry Weinberg

In the QCSD framework, it is recommended to conduct Product Coverage Sessions or Requirements Engineering Sessions on a regular basis. These sessions can be carried out at the epic level or for complex feature requests and user stories. Testers in the team can analyze the epic or feature story using SFDIPOT (a product factors checklist from Heuristic Test Strategy Model by James Bach) and come up with test ideas, questions about risks, missing information, unconsidered dependencies, identified risks, and more.

A guided discussion based on this analysis can help teams uncover hidden risks, assess the completeness of the requirements, create a clearer development plan, identify gaps and dependencies, improve estimation with better information at hand, and most importantly - avoid rework caused by discovering issues halfway through development.

If we want to save time and cost while still delivering quality software, it is always cheaper to do things right the first time. The purpose of this report is to facilitate Product Coverage Sessions and help teams achieve exactly that: doing things right the first time.

When to generate this report?

▼

The sooner the better! As soon as testers can access Epic/User Stories or any project artifact they use for test design, this report should be generated. Generate this report and organize "Product Coverage Session" discussion with relevant stakeholders such as programmers, Product Owners, Designers, Architects etc.

Risk-Based Prioritization

Test ideas are prioritized using a risk-based approach that considers:

Business Impact
Potential revenue loss, customer trust damage, or regulatory penalties
Likelihood of Failure
Complexity of implementation, external dependencies, new technology
User Exposure
Number of users affected and frequency of feature usage
Security & Compliance
Data protection requirements, legal obligations, industry regulations

Clarifying Questions to address potential coverage gaps

Since the user stories focus on product comparison architecture and switching journey components, the following subcategories require stakeholder input.

[Hardware]

Rationale: PMI targets global markets with varying device capabilities; minimum specs affect user reach.

  • What is the minimum device specification for the switching journey (RAM, processor), and what features degrade gracefully on lower-spec devices?
  • When a user's device cannot run the interactive comparison matrix, what is the fallback experience - simplified static comparison or redirect to support?
[Dependencies]

Rationale: Regional pricing and retailer APIs are external dependencies with varying SLAs across markets.

  • When a regional pricing provider goes offline during peak switching campaign, what is the maximum acceptable time before displaying "prices unavailable"?
  • If the age verification provider experiences a multi-hour outage, should the entire comparison feature be disabled or operate in a limited "informational only" mode?

Clarifying Questions to address potential coverage gaps

Since the user stories focus on product comparison, personalized planning, and cost calculations, the following functional aspects need stakeholder clarification.

[Business Rules - Health Claims]

Rationale: Tobacco industry faces strict regulatory scrutiny; health-related claims could trigger regulatory action.

  • If a health authority challenges our "potential savings" language as implying health benefits, who bears legal liability - and what is the approved fallback wording?
  • When a user's switching plan timeline contradicts medical guidance for their smoking history, should the system warn them or defer entirely to external resources?
[Calculations - Accuracy]

Rationale: Cost projections influence financial decisions; inaccurate calculations could damage trust and invite complaints.

  • What is the acceptable variance between calculated cost savings and actual market prices - and who is responsible when prices change mid-campaign?
  • When regional pricing data is more than 30 days old, should calculations show a "prices may have changed" warning or refuse to display projections?
[Security - Age Verification]

Rationale: Tobacco product marketing to minors carries severe legal penalties in most jurisdictions.

  • If our age verification provider is found to have falsely verified a minor, what is PMI's liability and what audit trail must we maintain?
  • What is the re-verification interval for returning users, and what happens to saved switching plans if verification expires?

Clarifying Questions to address potential coverage gaps

Since the user stories involve sensitive smoking habit data, cost calculations, and progress tracking, the following data handling aspects need clarification.

[Data Boundaries - Consumption Limits]

Rationale: Extreme smoking values could indicate data entry errors or edge cases requiring special handling.
